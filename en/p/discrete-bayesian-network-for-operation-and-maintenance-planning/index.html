Discrete Bayesian Network for Operation and Maintenance Planning

Mar 19, 2025
简体中文

RBI Modeling Methods

Different Models

  • Decision tree: Decision tree
  • Crude Monte Carlo simulations: Crude Monte Carlo simulations
  • Discrete Bayesian networks (dBN): Discrete Bayesian networks
  • Markov chain Monte Carlo simulation: Markov chain Monte Carlo simulation
  • Limited memory influence diagrams (LIMID): Limited memory influence diagrams
  • Partially observable Markov decision process: Partially observable Markov decision process

Model Comparison

  • MCMC does not require discretization of continuous variables, while dBN does
  • MCMC is more time-consuming than dBN because it is based on sampling for inference

Computational Framework in This Paper

Two Decision Models

  1. Bayesian network based: Uses Bayesian networks to directly estimate probabilities, suitable for simple decision processes (based on constants or observable measurements, such as regular inspection, where repair is performed when inspection results exceed a specified threshold)

  2. Simulation-based: Estimates probabilities through simulation, more time-consuming, but can be applied to more advanced strategies (based on variables updated with inspection and CM information, such as failure probability at the next time step)

Model Input Settings

  1. Specific cost: Costs of performing inspection, repair, and corrective repair
  2. Specification of models: Degradation probability model, repair probability model, inspection probability model, and monitoring probability model
  3. Strategy and parameters: Different maintenance strategies

Basic dBN Architecture

The dBN architecture shown below is mainly applicable to simulation-based models:

  • $M_0$: Represents initial model parameters; different model parameters can correspond to different types of degradation models
  • $D_0$: Represents initial damage state; generally, the initial state defaults to healthy and undamaged
  • $I_i$: Inspection result at time step $i$
  • $I_{M,i}$: Monitoring result at time step $i$
  • $I_{D,i}$: Decision on whether to perform inspection at time step $i$
  • $R_i$: Whether to perform repair at time step $i$, including two states (“repair” and “not repair”)
  • $D_{R,i}$: Damage state after repair or no repair at time step $i$

Probability Distribution Explanation

For example:

  • If there are $n_d$ structural damage states, then $P(D_0)$ should be a vector of length $n_d$
  • If there are $n_M$ structural parameters, then $P(M_0)$ should be a vector of length $n_M$
  • $P(M_i|M_{i-1})$ is an $n_M \times n_M$ matrix representing the change of model parameters in different time steps
  • $P(D_i|D_{R,i-1},M_i)$ is a three-dimensional matrix of size $n_D \times n_D \times n_M$
  • When the randomness of model parameters is not considered, for example, a fixed Markov chain as a degradation model, node $M_i$ can be omitted
  • $P(D_{R,i}|D_{i},R_i)$ is a three-dimensional matrix of size $n_D \times n_D \times 2$, representing the conditional probability distribution of $D_{R,i}$ given $D_i$ (damage state at time step $i$) and $R_i$ (whether to perform maintenance at time step $i$)
  • If there are $n_I$ inspection results, then the inspection model will be modeled in the form of $P(I_i|D_i,I_{D,i})$, with size $n_I \times n_D \times 2$
  • The health monitoring system is assumed to be related only to damage size $D_i$, expressed in the form of $P(I_{M,i}|D_i)$. If there are $n_{I_M}$ health monitoring system alarm results, then $P(I_{M,i}|D_i)$ is a matrix of size $n_{I_M} \times n_D$

Maintenance Strategies

This paper considers 13 different maintenance strategies:

  1. Corrective maintenance
  2. No inspection, periodic repair
  3. No inspection, determine whether to repair based on CM alarm results
  4. Equal interval inspection, determine whether to repair based on inspection results
  5. Determine whether to inspect based on CM alarm results, determine whether to repair based on inspection results
  6. No inspection, determine whether to repair based on failure probability
  7. Equal interval inspection, determine whether to repair based on failure probability
  8. Determine whether to inspect based on failure probability; determine whether to repair based on failure probability
  9. Determine whether to inspect based on failure probability; determine whether to repair based on inspection results
  10. No inspection, determine whether to repair based on expected value of damage size
  11. Equal interval inspection, determine whether to repair based on expected value of damage size
  12. Determine whether to inspect based on expected value of damage size, determine whether to repair based on expected value of damage size
  13. Determine whether to inspect based on expected value of damage size; determine whether to repair based on inspection results

Algorithm Explanation for Simple Strategies

Probability Update Formulas

Formula (1) updates the structural damage state probability at each time step: (Note that there are 6 variables here: $D_i, D_{i-1}, M_{i}, M_{i-1}, R_{i-1}$, and $D_{R,i-1}$. Therefore, the complete probability matrix will be a 6-dimensional matrix, consuming huge memory resources)

$$ P(D_i,M_i) = \sum_{D_{R,i-1}}\sum_{D_{i-1}}\sum_{R_{i-1}}\sum_{M_{i-1}} P(D_i|D_{R,i-1},M_i)P(D_{R,i-1}|D_{i-1},R_{i-1})\\ \times P(R_{i-1}|D_{i-1})P(M_i|M_{i-1})P(D_{i-1},M_{i-1}) $$

Formula (2) is the detailed representation of Formula (1) (taking 7 damage states and 3 model parameters as an example). It is worth explaining that the $\times$ in the formula here does not represent standard matrix multiplication, but represents probability product operation:

$$ P(D_i=d, M_i=m) = \sum_{d_{i-1}=0}^{6}\sum_{m_{i-1}=0}^{2}\sum_{r_{i-1}=0}^{1}\sum_{d_{r,i-1}=0}^{6} P(D_i=d|D_{R,i-1}=d_{r,i-1},M_i=m) \times P(D_{R,i-1}=d_{r,i-1}|D_{i-1}=d_{i-1},R_{i-1}=r_{i-1}) \times P(R_{i-1}=r_{i-1}|D_{i-1}=d_{i-1}) \times P(M_i=m|M_{i-1}=m_{i-1}) \times P(D_{i-1}=d_{i-1},M_{i-1}=m_{i-1}}) $$

Formula (3) is the formula after variable elimination, which can well improve computational efficiency and reduce memory resources, eliminating $M_{i-1}, R_{i-1}, D_{i-1}$ in sequence, and finally eliminating the $D_{R,i-1}$ variable:

$$ P(D_i,M_i) = \sum_{D_{R,i-1}} P(D_i|D_{R,i-1}, M_i) \sum_{D_{i-1}} \times \sum_{R_{i-1}} P(D_{R,i-1}|D_{i-1}, R_{i-1})P(R_{i-1}|D_{i-1}) \times \sum_{M_{i-1}} P(M_i|M_{i-1})P(D_{i-1}, M_{i-1}) $$

Failure and Repair Probabilities

  • Failure probability equals the probability that damage state $D_i$ equals “fault”
  • Repair probability is the probability that node $R_i$ takes the value “repair”
  • Need to formulate $P(R_i|D_i)$ according to different strategies. For example, for strategy 1, all $R_i$ are 0, i.e., no repair is performed

Algorithm Explanation for Complex Strategies

  • Randomly select $d_0$ from $P(D_0)$ distribution
  • Randomly select $m_0$ from $P(M_0)$ distribution
  • Calculate initial $P(D_0,M_0)=P(D_0)P(M_0)$
  • For each time step $i$:
    • Draw monitoring result $i_{M,i}$ from distribution $P(I_{M,i}|D_i=d_i)$
    • Update $P(D_i,M_i)$ using Bayesian criterion ;*
    • Make decision on whether to inspect according to decision rule $i_{D,i}$
    • Draw inspection result $i_i$ from distribution $P(I_{i}|D_i=d_i, I_{D,i}=i_{D,i})$
    • Update $P(D_i,M_i)$ using Bayesian criterion ;*
    • Make decision on whether to repair according to decision rule $r_i$
    • Draw structural damage state after repair $d_{R,i}$ from distribution $P(D_{R,i}|D_i=d_i, R_{i}=r_{i})$
    • Draw next step damage state $d_{i+1}$ from distribution $P(D_{i+1}|D_{R,i}=d_{R,i}, M_{i+1}=m_{i+1})$
    • Update $P(D_{i+1},M_{i+1})$ according to degradation and repair algorithm ; *

(* indicates that Bayesian theorem needs to be used for probability update)
