Structural Health Monitoring of Offshore Structures Using Sensor Data: In-Depth Analysis of PSA Research Report

Feb 25, 2025
简体中文

Series Introduction: This series of articles provides in-depth interpretation of reports and standards related to offshore structural health monitoring and management, covering the design and implementation of Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) systems, Risk-Based Inspection (RBI), and Structural Integrity Management (SIM). This inaugural article presents a detailed analysis of the research report on structural health monitoring of offshore structures using sensor data by the Petroleum Safety Authority Norway (PSA).

Executive Summary

Basic Information

  1. Project Name: PSA study: Structural health monitoring of offshore structure by use of sensor data

  2. Publication Date: February 21, 2024

  3. Project Objectives:

    • Explore how SHM can serve as a decision support tool for assessing the health of offshore structures
    • Investigate how to use SHM (including sensor data, structural failure history, etc.) to guide operation and maintenance decisions
    • Evaluate and compare the advantages and disadvantages of different SHM approaches
    • Present case studies of offshore structures using SHM for maintenance planning

“This report explores structural health monitoring (SHM) as a decision support tool to assess the structural health of offshore structures. The main objective of this report is to evaluate how SHM may be used to improve safety and inspection and maintenance planning with use of sensor data and structural failure history. The report evaluates different SHM approaches, including strengths and weaknesses, and contains some examples of use of SHM for both maintenance planning and to capture safety critical behavior of offshore structures.”

Key Terminology

  1. SHM (Structural Health Monitoring): A diagnostic tool used to detect structural failures, which may also include functions such as warning, localizing, and evaluating the consequences of structural failures.

“SHM is a diagnostic tool that has the objective to detect a structural failure, and may in addition warn, localize and evaluate the consequence of a structural failure.”

  1. Digital Twin: A virtual representation of a system or asset that calculates system states and makes system information available through integrated models and data, with the purpose of providing decision support over its life cycle.

“A digital twin is a virtual representation of a system or asset that calculates system states and makes system information available, through integrated models and data, with the purpose of providing decision support over its life cycle.”

  1. Inspection: The process of examination, measurement, and/or testing to determine the condition or state of an item.

“Process of examination, measurement and/or testing to determine a condition or state of an item”

  1. Virtual Sensors: Numerical sensors or sensors derived based on physical sensor data.

“Virtual sensors are numerical sensors or derived based on physical sensors”

  1. Monitoring System: A system that receives information about the system through measuring devices (sensors) and makes it available to the operator.

“A monitoring system receives information about the system through measuring devices (sensors), and makes it available to the operator”

  1. Risk-Based Inspection (RBI): A process that identifies, assesses, and maps industrial risks (due to corrosion and stress cracking) that could compromise equipment integrity in both pressurized equipment and structural elements. RBI addresses risks that can be controlled through proper inspections and analysis. During the RBI process, engineers design inspection strategies (what, when, how to inspect) that most efficiently match forecasted or observed degradation mechanisms.

“Risk-based inspection (RBI) is a process that identifies, assesses and maps industrial risks (due to corrosion and stress cracking), which could compromise equipment integrity in both pressurized equipment and structural elements. RBI addresses risks that can be controlled through proper inspections and analysis. During the RBI process, engineers design inspection strategies (what, when, how to inspect) that most efficiently match forecasted or observed degradation mechanisms.”

SHM System Implementation Process

The standard process for establishing an SHM system includes the following phases:

Phase 1: Assess Structural Operating Environment

  • Identify structural failure modes, degradation mechanisms, and their consequences
  • Select appropriate sensors, installation schemes, and cost budgets

Phase 2: Build SHM System

  • Sensor installation, calibration, and data logging system configuration
  • System integration testing
  • Data recording, filtering, compression, and transmission

Phase 3: Data Analysis and Processing

  • Fault diagnosis, damage identification
  • Modal analysis

Phase 4: Structural Integrity Assessment

  • Structural condition assessment
  • Remaining life prediction

Can SHM Prevent Major Accidents?

Case 1: Alexander Kjelland Platform Capsizing Accident (1981)

Norway’s largest industrial accident was the capsizing of the Alexander Kjelland platform in 1981. The structural damage originated from a 6mm fillet weld defect, followed by fatigue crack propagation in the support structure, leading to the failure of one of the platform’s legs.

SHM System Applicability Analysis in This Scenario:

  • Limitations: Using sensor data-based SHM systems might not have prevented this accident, as the platform was a non-redundant structure where single-point failure led to overall capsizing.

  • Potential Value:

    • SHM can support decision-making and reduce the likelihood of accidents
    • The crack would have to be detected before it developed into critical damage to potentially prevent the accident
    • However, the crack location was not a critical part assessed during the design phase, and sensors would typically not be placed there
    • Only if the sensor system could detect cracks larger than 100mm and recognize that such a crack would develop into critical damage could the accident potentially have been prevented

Case 2: Ekofisk 2/4 W Platform Ship Collision Accident (2009)

In the 2009 ship collision accident at the Ekofisk 2/4 W platform in Norway, the value of SHM was manifested in:

  • Decision Support Tool: SHM can serve as a decision support tool for assessing structural damage, providing preliminary assessment before more detailed inspection

  • Real-time Safety Assessment:

    • Providing real-time structural load-bearing capacity assessment
    • Assessing remaining life
    • Determining whether personnel can be arranged to enter the structure after collision for inspection

  • Technical Requirements: Achieving the above functions requires the SHM system to be able to monitor large deformations caused by ship collision

Structural Integrity Management (SIM)

SIM Overview

Structure Lifecycle

Structural integrity management covers the entire lifecycle of offshore structures from design, construction, and installation to operation, maintenance, and decommissioning.

SIM Definition

Structural Integrity Management (SIM) is a continuous process for managing the integrity status of offshore structures from installation to decommissioning throughout their lifecycle. SIM provides a systematic management framework for structural inspection planning, maintenance, and repair planning.

SIM Framework Core Modules

The framework mainly includes the following four core modules:

  1. Data and Information Collection: Gathering structural monitoring data, inspection records, maintenance history, and other information

  2. Structural Condition Assessment: Evaluating the current health status of the structure based on collected data

  3. Maintenance Strategy Evaluation and Selection: Developing and selecting optimal maintenance and repair strategies

  4. Inspection Plan Establishment: Developing risk-based or condition-based inspection plans

Barrier Management

Core Concepts

Concept Definition

Barrier Element Technical, operational, or organizational measures or solutions that implement barrier functions
Barrier Function The task or role undertaken by a barrier in the event chain from faults, hazards, or unexpected situations leading to accidents
Performance Influencing Factors Conditions that significantly affect the operation of barrier functions and elements as expected

Inspection Planning

Core Questions

The primary goal of inspection planning is to determine four key questions: what, when, where, and how to inspect.

Population-based SHM

As shown in the figure above, population-based SHM is an important component of SIM. Taking wind farms as an example, selecting representative wind turbines in the farm (usually those with the highest utilization or load), and collecting monitoring information from these structures to guide asset management strategies for other turbines in the farm.

Inspection Technology Evaluation Metrics

Probability of Detection (PoD)

When selecting inspection technologies, the Probability of Detection (PoD) should be considered, which measures the accuracy of inspection technologies.

Trueness and Precision

Metric Definition
Trueness The degree of deviation between measurement results and true values
Precision The consistency of independent inspection results

Classification Model Evaluation Metrics

For classification models, the following metrics can be used for evaluation:

  • Confusion matrix
  • AUC (Area Under Curve)
  • Recall rate
  • Classifier accuracy
  • F1 score

Inspection Timing Selection

When to inspect should be based on a combination of Time-Based Inspection (TBI) and Condition-Based Inspection (CBI):

  • TBI should be set based on RBI results
  • As SHM system information increases, TBI can gradually be replaced by CBI

Data-driven Inspection Planning

Regulatory Basis

According to Norwegian standard Norsok N-005, inspection plans should consider risk-based inspection analysis results.

RBI Implementation Methods

Risk-Based Inspection (RBI) can be implemented at the following three levels:

Level Method Characteristics
Generic Design Basis Using historical data or engineering experience from the same type or similar structures Inspection scope based on conservative assumptions for operational use
Qualitative Analysis Combining historical data or engineering experience from the same or similar structures, with specific design details Inspection scope more targeted to concentrate on special areas with high utilization
Quantitative Analysis Probabilistic analysis based on specific design Most precise method, requiring detailed structural information and reliability data

Analysis of Common Offshore Structural Failures

Classification of Structural Limit States

Limit State Full English Name Definition Focus Areas Objectives
SLS Serviceability Limit State Functional requirements that structures should meet under normal service conditions Deformation, vibration, crack width, etc. Ensure structural functionality and comfort
FLS Fatigue Limit State Ability of structures to resist fatigue failure under cyclic loading Cyclic stress, fatigue life, crack propagation, etc. Prevent fatigue failure caused by cyclic loading
ULS Ultimate Limit State Ability of structures to resist failure under maximum loading Strength, stability, overall stability, etc. Ensure safety under extreme conditions, prevent collapse or severe damage
ALS Accidental Limit State Performance of structures under accidental events (such as explosion, impact, fire, etc.) Residual load-bearing capacity, overall stability, local damage, etc. Ensure no catastrophic failure occurs during accidental events, minimize losses
