IEC 61400-28 Asset Management Standard Interpretation

Apr 15, 2025
简体中文

Process Overview

Core Principles

  • Decisions should be based on a thorough risk management process
  • Inspection/maintenance schemes should be reviewed and optimized (initiated if not initially present)
  • Condition monitoring schemes should be reviewed and optimized (initiated if initially inadequate for assessment of asset health with respect to the most significant risks or modes of failure, at sufficiently frequent time intervals)

Six-Step Implementation Process

Step 1: Historical Data and Maintenance Status Acquisition

  • Site characterization data: Wind speed and turbulence are particularly important
  • Any information about replacement parts, maintenance history, and load history is very important
  • Annex B describes which information is important for assessing the loading history of components
  • Clause 5 gives general recommendations regarding collection and storage of data generated during the asset lifetime. Recommendations are also made regarding sources and categories of information.

Step 2: Risk Management Process

  • Risk-based: Before conducting asset management, assessment, classification, and ranking should be performed (different O&M strategies for different risks)
  • Clause 6 provides guidance on the assessment and ranking of risks. A risk assessment will help to prioritize efforts in the further steps.

Step 3: Operation, Maintenance and Inspection

  • A maintenance and inspection scheme can be drawn up, as described in Clause 7
  • For operating wind turbines, if no maintenance records exist, it is assumed that relevant equipment maintenance measures have not been implemented
  • Update risk rankings from Step 2 and estimate RUL based on inspection information

Step 4: Condition Monitoring (Clause 8)

Condition monitoring can provide continuously updated asset information, which can be used for:

  • Updating numerical models
  • Optimizing O&M operations
  • Predicting and detecting structural failure probabilities
  • Collecting data for reliability statistical analysis and planning Inspection & Maintenance
  • Determining whether structural life can be extended

Can provide more inputs for risk assessment, thereby further optimizing inspection and O&M schemes.

Step 6: Analytical Assessment of RUL

Asset remaining useful life analysis methods can range from simple proportional estimation to absolute assessment:

First Method (Relative Assessment):

  • Relies on proportional functions or ratios (applied to equivalent damage loads)
  • First derived using design-time parameters
  • Then derived using site-specific operational parameters
  • This method may be suitable when available models, assumptions, or parameters are not sufficiently clear or cannot be verified

Second Method (Absolute Assessment):

  • Only applicable when characteristics, operating conditions, machines, and model definitions are accurate (Clause 10)

Important Principles:

  • Analytical assessments should always be accompanied by estimates of uncertainty and accuracy, covering input data and the computational model itself
  • Annex D describes some probability-based models, some of which are derived from measured loads. These models aim to make full use of historical data to improve estimates of remaining useful life for specific components and structures

Clause 5: Data Management, Requirements and Uncertainty

Missing Data Reconstruction

There are benefits to filling gaps in measured operating data. Not all data are captured comprehensively during the lifetime of each individual turbine. Strategies exist to address the issue of regenerating missing data retrospectively, based on scrutiny of data from turbines of similar design, site conditions, maintenance history and operational philosophy.

Data/Information Category Definitions

Dynamic Data

Data that changes rapidly during wind turbine operation, such as:

  • Wind speed, rotational speed, angle, frequency, temperature, etc.
  • Condition monitoring data (CM data), such as vibration signals, corrosion depth, oil analysis, crack depth, etc.

Important Requirements:

  • Need to assess data and model uncertainty
  • For measurement data, uncertainty should be estimated according to ISO/IEC Guide 98-3
  • Measurement data uncertainty must be assessed

Static Data

Such as inspection reports, manuals, etc. This type of data does not have uncertainty but tolerances, i.e., allowable variations in thicknesses, diameters, rated power, weights, center of gravity (CoG) positions, moments of inertia, etc. Such tolerances can induce variance into the assessment by altering the strength or the dynamic loads.

CM Data Applications

CM data can be used for:

  • Measuring the accuracy of predictive assessment methods (e.g., determining whether predicted cracks or corrosion depths are accurate)
  • Monitoring structural degradation rates where life cannot be predicted
  • As a trigger factor for inspection intervention measures

Wind Measurement Requirements

  • Measurement interval: Should be at 10-minute intervals
  • Ideal setup: Use multiple instruments installed on different meteorological towers, at different heights including hub height
  • Measurement period: Should be at least 6 months and completed before wind farm installation
  • Seasonal considerations: If seasonal variations significantly affect wind conditions, the monitoring period should be at least 12 months to cover these effects
  • Data content: Should include summary statistics for wind speed, wind direction, temperature and atmospheric pressure (for all parameters, at least including mean, minimum, maximum and standard deviation)

SCADA Data (IEC 61400-25-1 and IEC 61400-25-2)

Strongly recommended recorded information:

  • Working hours
  • Alarm, warning and operational logs
  • Nacelle orientation
  • Wind direction at nacelle wind vane
  • Temperature of main components (average, maximum, minimum)

Fault Statistical Analysis

When common failures occur in wind farms (or multiple wind farms), appropriate statistical analysis should be conducted to establish statistical models for predicting the likelihood of future failures. For example, statistical distributions (such as Weibull distribution or Weibull Bayesian distribution) can be fitted to the data. This will help in maintenance planning and spare parts reserves.

Note: If the dataset provided for a specific combination of component, failure mode and site conditions is small (e.g., fewer wind turbines at a specific site), these models will have greater uncertainty (Clause 10).

Inspection Records and Reports

Can include the following:

  • Structural integrity (e.g., dye penetrant or NDE for crack detection)
  • Boroscopy (e.g., main bearing, gearbox bearings, gears)
  • Dimensional (e.g., wear, free movement, play, backlash)
  • Visual (e.g., leakage, overheating, loose/missing/fractured bolts, corrosion, dents, stress raisers)
  • Bolt tension (e.g., re-torquing)
  • Grease and oil sample analyses

IEC 61400-13

Load measurement standards.

Data Uncertainty Level Definition

Can be defined based on the ratio of standard deviation to mean.

Clause 10: Analytical Assessment of Turbine Lifetime

Relative Assessment

Loads under site conditions are compared with loads calculated according to certified design conditions. These loads are calculated using aero-elastic models.

Through the obtained load ratios, remaining useful life (RUL) can be derived using appropriate damage models (e.g., comparing equivalent damage loads using simple S-N curves).

Characteristics:

  • Design margins are generally not considered
  • Design margins may only be considered when appropriate design documentation is available
  • Special attention is needed to the correctness and completeness of design documentation, as well as the accuracy of relative assessment
  • Due to its relative nature, this method is less sensitive to model errors than the absolute method described below

Implementation Steps:

  1. Use aero-elastic models (can be verified through load measurements)
  2. Calculate relative remaining useful life (RUL) based on design conditions and site-specific wind conditions
  3. Fully estimate RUL uncertainty
  4. Based on obtained RUL, uncertainty, and risk assessment procedures described in Clause 6, formulate inspection, maintenance and monitoring strategies

Absolute Assessment

Aims to determine absolute damage of components to estimate their remaining useful life (RUL).

Characteristics:

  • Compared with relative assessment, the damage models used are more complex
  • Requires more data and information about the wind turbine or components
  • Due to the use of absolute values rather than relative values, this method is more sensitive to model inaccuracies (or measurement inaccuracies in the case of load measurements)
  • Should only be used when there is accurate understanding of the physical and geometric characteristics of the wind turbine or components, as well as the wind turbine control and safety systems

Implementation Steps:

  1. Use well-verified aero-elastic models and component models, e.g., verified through load measurements
  2. Calculate absolute remaining useful life (RUL) based on site-specific wind conditions
  3. Fully estimate RUL uncertainty
  4. Based on obtained RUL, uncertainty, and risk assessment procedures described in Clause 6, formulate inspection, maintenance and monitoring strategies

Uncertainty Assessment

Should include at least the following aspects:

  • Environmental data (e.g., atmospheric turbulence, wind speed distribution, wave distribution)
  • Aero-elastic models (aerodynamic calculation models)
  • Measurement data
  • Fatigue damage models (e.g., application of simplified damage equivalent loads, S-N curve parameters, theories of crack initiation, growth, and possible application of fracture mechanics)

Failure Mode Definition: Relevant failure modes for structural or major components shall be defined in accordance with IEC 61400-1, for example, fatigue crack initiation of fibre or matrix in the spar caps of the blade structure.
